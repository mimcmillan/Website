Complex numbers

Complex algebra

Complex numbers are combinations of real numbers and imaginary numbers. Every complex number can be written in this ‘rectangular’ form: z=a+bi,\qquad a,\,b\in\mathbb{R}

Complex sums and products

  • ! Treat ‘i’ like an indefinite variable symbol ‘x’.
    • Complex numbers are added, subtracted, scaled, and multiplied like polynomials in the letter i.
  • !! Simplify powers of i using i^2=-1.
    • For example: \begin{gather*}(1+3i)(2-2i)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 2-2i+6i-6i^2\\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad2+4i-6(-1)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad8+4i\end{gather*}

Example 15-01

Complex products

Problem:
Compute the products:

  • (a) (1-i)(4-7i)
  • (b) (2+5i)(2-5i)

Solution:
(a) (1-i)(4-7i)

  1. & Expand.
    • Distribute: (1-i)(4-7i)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 4-7i-4i+7i^2
  2. && Substitute i^2\; \rightsquigarrow \; -1.
    • Insert -1 for i^2: ⨠⨠ \quad 4-7i-4i+7(-1)
    • Simplify: ⨠⨠ \quad -3-11i

(b) (2+5i)(2-5i)

  1. & Expand.
    • Distribute: (2+5i)(2-5i)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 4-10i+10i-25i^2
  2. & Substitute i^2\rightsquigarrow -1.
    • Insert -1 for i^2: ⨠⨠ \quad 4-10i+10i-25(-1)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 29
  3. !! Notice this is a real number!

Conjugates
Any z=a+bi has its complex conjugate that is \bar{z}=a-bi.

For example, the conjugate of 2+5i is 2-5i. The conjugate of 2-5i is 2+5i.

  • ! Notice that \bar{\bar{z}}=z.

The main useful feature of conjugates is their power to annihilate imaginary parts:
(2+5i)(2-5i)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 4+25\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 29In general:
(a+bi)(a-bi)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad a^2-abi+bia-b^2i^2\quad ⨠⨠ \quad a^2+b^2

  • !! The complex conjugate creates pure real numbers via multiplication: \bar{z}z\in\mathbb{R}

This feature is critical for computing complex division.

Division by complex numbers

  • & Division in terms of \bar{z}.
    • Multiply above and below by \bar{z}: \begin{gather*}\frac{1}{z}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{z}\cdot\frac{\bar{z}}{\bar{z}}\\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{\bar{z}}{z\bar{z}}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{\bar{z}z}\cdot\bar{z}\end{gather*}
    • The expression \displaystyle\frac{1}{\bar{z}z} is a real number that scales both coefficients of \bar{z}.
  • ! Division in terms of a+bi.
    • Multiply above and below by a-bi: \begin{gather*}\frac{1}{a+bi}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{a+bi}\cdot\frac{a-bi}{a-bi}\\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{a-bi}{a^2+b^2}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \left(\frac{a}{a^2+b^2}\right)+\left(\frac{-b}{a^2+b^2}\right)i\end{gather*}
    • The last expression is in the standard ‘rectangular’ format for complex numbers.
  • && Division of a+bi by c+di.
    • Multiply above and below by the denominator conjugate: \begin{gather*}\frac{a+bi}{c+di}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{a+bi}{c+di}\cdot\frac{c-di}{c-di}\\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{ac+bd+(bc-ad)i}{c^2+d^2}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{ac+bd}{c^2+d^2}+\frac{bc-ad}{c^2+d^2}i\end{gather*}
    • This is faster than computing \frac{1}{c+di} first and then multiplying the result by a+bi.
  • ! Note a general rule that \overline{zw}=\bar{z}\bar{w} for any two complex numbers z and w.
    • Conjugation and multiplication can be done in either order.

Example 15-02

Complex division

Problem:
Compute the following divisions of complex numbers:

  • (a) \displaystyle\frac{1}{-3+i}
  • (b) \displaystyle\frac{1}{i}
  • (c) \displaystyle\frac{1}{7i}
  • (d) \displaystyle\frac{2+5i}{2-5i}

Solution:
(a) \displaystyle\frac{1}{-3+i}

  1. && Multiply above and below by the conjugate.
    • Conjugate is -3-i: \frac{1}{-3+i}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{-3+i}\cdot\frac{-3-i}{-3-i}
    • Simplify: ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{-3-i}{9+1}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{-3}{10}+\frac{-1}{10}i

(b) \displaystyle\frac{1}{i}

  1. & Multiply above and below by the conjugate.
    • Conjugate is -i: \frac{1}{i}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{i}\cdot\frac{-i}{-i}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad -i

(c) \displaystyle\frac{1}{7i}

  1. && Rewrite in terms of 1/i.
    • Factor out the 1/7: \frac{1}{7i}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{7}\cdot\frac{1}{i}
    • Use \frac{1}{i}=-i from (b): ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{1}{7}\cdot(-i)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{-1}{7}i

(d) \displaystyle\frac{2+5i}{2-5i}

  1. && Multiply above and below by the conjugate.
    • Denominator conjugate is 2+5i: \frac{2+5i}{2-5i}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{2+5i}{2-5i}\cdot\frac{2+5i}{2+5i}
    • Simplify: ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{4+20i+25i^2}{4+25}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{-21}{29}+\frac{20}{29}i

Complex numbers - polar and exponential forms

The complex numbers written so far have been in ‘rectangular’ form. Next we consider ‘polar’ form. The reason to do so is that multiplication is much easier to understand in polar form.

The polar form of a+bi is written like this: \begin{align*}a+bi &= r\cos\theta+r\sin\theta\, i \\\\ &= r\big(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta\big)\\\\&= r\,\mathrm{cis}\,\theta\end{align*}where (r,\theta) is a polar coordinate pair indicating the point given in Cartesian coordinates by (a,b).

  • ! The acronym ‘\mathrm{cis}(\theta)’ stands for \cos\theta + i \sin\theta.

For example:
\begin{gather*}\sqrt{2}-\sqrt{2}i \quad ⨠⨠ \quad \sqrt{2}\,(1-i)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 2\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}i\right)\\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad 2\cos\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)+2\sin\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)\,i\end{gather*}which we may also write: =2\,\mathrm{cis}\left(-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)

Euler Formula

The Euler Formula links complex polar to exponentiation: e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta

For complex numbers not on the unit circle, incorporate a radius: re^{i\theta}=r\cos\theta+r\sin\theta\,i

The main application of this formula is to use exponent rules to help interpret complex multiplication.

Example 15-03

Complex products, quotients, powers using Euler’s Formula
  • &&& Consider the complex numbers z=2e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}} and w=5e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}.
    • Their product zw is: \begin{gather*}zw \quad ⨠⨠ \quad \left(2e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}\right)\cdot\left(5e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}\right) \\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad (2\cdot 5)\left(e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}\right)\left(e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}\right)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 10e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}+i\frac{\pi}{3}}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 10e^{i\frac{5\pi}{6}}\end{gather*}
    • Their quotient z/w is: \begin{gather*}z/w \quad ⨠⨠ \quad \left(2e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}\right)\Big/\left(5e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}\right) \\\\ ⨠⨠ \frac{2e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}}{5e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{2}{5}e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}e^{-i\frac{\pi}{3}}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \frac{2}{5}e^{i\frac{\pi}{6}}\end{gather*}
    • The power z^8 is: \begin{gather*}z^8\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \left(2e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}\right)^8\\\\ ⨠⨠ \quad 2^8\left(e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}\right)^8\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 512e^{i\cdot4\pi}\end{gather*}
      • Notice: e^{i\cdot4\pi}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \left(e^{2\pi i}\right)^2\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 1^2\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 1
      • Simplify: 512e^{i\cdot 4\pi}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 512
      • ! Thus: z^8=512.
  • && Compute (3+3i)^4.
    • First convert to exponential: 3+3i\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 3\sqrt{2}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}+\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}i\right)\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 3\sqrt{2}e^{i\frac{\pi}{4}}
    • Next compute the power: (3+3i)^4\quad ⨠⨠ \quad \left(3\sqrt{2}e^{i\frac{\pi}{4}}\right)^4\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 324e^{i\pi}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad -324
Complex multiplication rule

In general, we have the rule: r_1e^{i\theta_1}\cdot r_2e^{i\theta_2} = (r_1r_2)e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)}

  • Complex multiplication: multiply radii and add angles.

In light of that rule, can you understand this statement?

  • ! Multiplication by e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}} means: rotate counterclockwise by 90^\circ.

Also, notice the fact that e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}=+i. So i ‘acts upon’ other numbers by rotating them by 90^\circ counterclockwise.

The multiplication rule leads to a powers rule in polar notation:

De Moivre’s Theorem

In general, we have the rule: \big(re^{i\theta}\big)^n=r^ne^{i\cdot n\theta}

Written in polar notation, this means: \big(r\,\mathrm{cis}\,\theta\big)^n = r^n\,\mathrm{cis}\,(n\theta)or, expanded out: \big(r\cos\theta+r\sin\theta\,i\big)^n = r^n\cos(n\theta)+r^n\sin(n\theta)\,i

Complex roots

The exponential notation leads to a formula for a complex n^\text{th} root of any complex number:
\big(re^{i\theta}\big)^{\frac{1}{n}} = r^{\frac{1}{n}}e^{i\frac{\theta}{n}}

  • !!! However, every complex number has n distinct complex n^\text{th} roots!
Formula for all n of the n^\text{th} roots of z=re^{i\theta}

The roots are given by: w_k = r^\frac{1}{n}e^{i\left(\frac{\theta}{n}+k\frac{2\pi}{n}\right)} for k=0,\,1,\,2,\,\dots,\,n-1.

Therefore w_k^n=z for every k=0,\,1,\,2,\,\dots,\,n-1.

In Cartesian notation, these are given by: w_k = r^\frac{1}{n}\cos\left(\frac{\theta}{n}+k\frac{2\pi}{n}\right)+r^\frac{1}{n}\sin\left(\frac{\theta}{n}+k\frac{2\pi}{n}\right)i

Example 15-04

Finding all the 4^\text{th} roots of 16

Problem:
Compute all the 4^\text{th} roots of 16.

Solution:

  1. & Write 16=16e^{0i}.
  2. & Evaluate roots formula.
    • Roots: \left(16e^{0i}\right)^\frac{1}{4}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad w_k=16^\frac{1}{4}e^{i\left(\frac{0}{4}+k\frac{2\pi}{4}\right)}
    • Simplify: ⨠⨠ \quad 2e^{i\cdot k\frac{\pi}{2}}\quad ⨠⨠ \quad 2,\;2i,\;-2,\;-2i
